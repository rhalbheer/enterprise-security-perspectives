### Implementation Plan

- Define risk appetite and thresholds with executive leadership.

- Begin quarterly risk assessments and policy reviews.

- Formalize Collective Defense in governance by establishing secure channels with governments, universities, and hyperscalers and trusted partners for threat intelligence sharing.

- Implement basic AI governance policies (approved tools, data restrictions).

- Start data classification and inventory for sensitive assets.

##### Identity / Zero Trust

- Enforce MFA everywhere (including privileged accounts).

- Deploy phishing-resistant MFA for high-risk roles.

### Required Competencies for a Strategic CISO

#### Strategic Business Alignment & Risk Translation

- Align security with business objectives: Modern CISOs are expected to act as partners to the enterprise, ensuring cybersecurity supports business success rather than hinders it. This means framing security goals and risks in terms of business impact (e.g. revenue protection, reputational integrity, regulatory compliance) and integrating cyber risk management into corporate strategy. A CISO should translate technical threats into business language that executive management and boards can readily understand, highlighting how resilience and security enable competitive advantage and continuity.

- Enable smart risk-taking: Rather than saying *“no”* to new initiatives, effective CISOs find secure ways to achieve business goals. They focus on acceptable risk – helping the organization take smart risks by applying controls that mitigate threats to an acceptable level. This approach positions the CISO as a facilitator of innovation (e.g. secure digital transformation) with resilience as a driver for growth, instead of being perceived as a roadblock.

#### Technical & Risk Management Expertise

- Deep cybersecurity knowledge: Authoritative standards emphasize that a CISO must have a strong technical foundation. This includes mastery of cybersecurity frameworks and best practices (such as the NIST Cybersecurity Framework and ISO/IEC 27001 for information security management) and familiarity with risk management methodologies. Such expertise allows the CISO to design and maintain an effective security program and Information Security Management System (ISMS) aligned with business needs.

- Risk management & compliance: The CISO should be skilled in identifying, assessing, and treating cyber risks in business terms. Using standards like NIST or ISO risk frameworks, the CISO develops a comprehensive cyber risk strategy and ensures it is regularly updated and reviewed by leadership. They must also ensure compliance with relevant regulations and laws (e.g. GDPR, NIS2, industry-specific standards), and integrate those requirements into the enterprise risk management processes. This competence in governance and risk management ensures that security measures support the organization’s risk appetite and legal obligations.

#### Leadership & Communication

- Executive influence: A high-performing CISO combines technical know-how with strong leadership skills. They need to effectively communicate and influence at the executive level. This means clearly explaining cybersecurity issues – from emerging threats to “ROI” of security investments – in non-technical, business-focused terms that resonate with CEOs and board members. By discussing security in terms of financial, operational, and reputational risk, the CISO secures senior leadership buy-in and treats cyber risk as a core business risk.

- Negotiation & advocacy: Strategic CISOs are adept at negotiation and advocacy within the organization. They can justify and obtain sufficient budget and resources for security initiatives by linking them to business value and risk reduction. They champion a culture of security, working with other leaders to embed cyber risk awareness across all departments. Through regular briefings, training, and collaborative governance, the CISO ensures that cybersecurity remains a top priority supported by company leadership.

#### Collaboration & Solution-Oriented Approach

- Cross-functional collaboration: Rather than operating in isolation, an effective CISO works closely with other business units (IT, finance, operations, etc.) to embed security into processes and projects from the start. This collaborative approach helps in integrating security into digital initiatives seamlessly, ensuring that teams collectively uphold resilience objectives. By building partnerships across the organization, the CISO helps others understand security as a shared responsibility and business enabler.

- Adaptability and proactive problem-solving: Successful security leaders are adaptable and solution oriented. They anticipate emerging threats and regulatory changes and adjust strategies accordingly. Importantly, they present solutions – not just problems – when confronted with business requests. For example, if a new product poses certain risks, a solution-oriented CISO will offer alternative safe approaches or compensating controls instead of simply vetoing the project. This proactive stance avoids the “nay-sayer” stigma and demonstrates that cybersecurity can support innovation and resilience simultaneously.

### Checklist for an MSSP and SOC

#### Checklist Overview

The checklist is structured around 10 critical domains derived from the "Security at Machine Speed" paper, emphasizing that modern cybersecurity is a race run in minutes, not days. Each domain contains strategic questions designed to reveal whether a SOC/MSSP can support business resilience, align with regulatory expectations, and translate security operations into executive-level risk metrics.

The questions recognize that minutes now define cyber risk. Breaches can escalate and exfiltrate at machine speed, with breakout times collapsed to less than an hour and ransomware operators exfiltrating data within five hours of entry. Your SOC or MSSP must demonstrate they can match this speed.

#### Domain 1: Governance & Risk Management

Why This Domain Matters: Security governance is the mechanism that turns technical insight into explicit business choices. At machine speed, governance must run on the same truth as operations, establishing who owns which risks, how decisions are made, and how control effectiveness is measured continuously.

##### Strategic Questions

- How does the SOC/MSSP support continuous, business-aligned security governance with board-level visibility?
  - Strategic Context: Boards require visibility into cyber risk as enterprise risk. Continuous governance ensures decisions are informed, documented, and defensible.

- How do you define and measure risk appetite, align it to our business objectives, and embed it in your operating procedures?
  - Strategic Context: Risk appetite defines what level of exposure is acceptable to achieve business speed. Misalignment creates either excessive risk or paralysis.

- How do you ensure control effectiveness is measured continuously rather than through periodic audits?
  - Strategic Context: In a machine-speed threat environment, annual audits are insufficient. Continuous monitoring proves controls are working in real-time.

- What frameworks and processes do you use to ensure compliance with regulations (e.g., NIS2, DORA, GDPR, AI Act)?
  - Strategic Context: Regulatory expectations impose time-bound obligations (24-72 hour notification deadlines). Non-compliance carries financial and reputational consequences.

- How do you monitor data classification, lifecycle management, and DLP across hybrid and multi-cloud environments?
  - Strategic Context: Data is the crown jewel. Without classification and lifecycle governance, you cannot protect what matters most or prove compliance.

- What is your approach to third-party and supply-chain risk management, including monitoring and incident coordination?
  - Strategic Context: Your attack surface includes your ecosystem. A compromised supplier can become the entry point for a breach, as demonstrated by the Midnight Blizzard attack on Microsoft.


#### Domain 2: Visibility & Telemetry (Security Data Lake)

Why This Domain Matters: To turn board-level risk decisions into action, leaders need a shared, real-time view of the digital terrain. A unified security data lake and relationship-aware security graph provide "one map, different zoom levels" from board oversight to SOC response.

##### Strategic Questions:

- Do you operate a unified security data lake that consolidates telemetry from identity, endpoint, cloud, network, and SaaS sources?
  - Strategic Context: Fragmented telemetry creates blind spots. A unified data lake is the foundation for detection, investigation, and evidence-based reporting.

- How do you ensure log coverage, data quality, and retention aligned with both operational and regulatory requirements?
  - Strategic Context: Poor log quality or retention gaps prevent forensic investigation and regulatory reporting. Logs must be both operational and evidential.

- Can you describe your implementation of a relationship-aware security graph that connects identities, devices, applications, data, and business services?
  - Strategic Context: Attackers think in graphs (relationships), not lists. A security graph reveals attack paths and choke points that traditional tools miss.

- How do you provide different "zoom levels" of visibility for board oversight, CISO steering, and SOC operations?
  - Strategic Context: Different stakeholders need different altitudes: board sees exposure to critical services; CISO sees risk trends; SOC sees real-time incidents. \[(30 p) Sec...ve Edition \| Word\]

- What blind spots commonly exist in organizations, and how do you identify and close them (e.g., shadow IT, shadow AI, forgotten cloud resources)?
  - Strategic Context: Shadow IT, shadow AI, and orphaned cloud resources are invisible to traditional tools. They are common entry points for attackers.

- How quickly can you map attack paths from entry points to crown-jewel assets, and what visibility gaps would prevent this?
  - Strategic Context: Speed of containment depends on understanding blast radius. If you cannot map paths quickly, you cannot contain quickly.


#### Domain 3: Identity as the Perimeter

Why This Domain Matters: Identity is now the real perimeter. Most intrusions start with credentials, tokens, or consent. Credential theft, MFA fatigue, and social engineering are precision tools for rapid compromise.

##### Strategic Questions:

- How do you enforce phishing-resistant MFA for all identities in the SOC?
  - Strategic Context: Identity is the new perimeter. Credential compromise is the most common initial access vector, bypassing traditional network defenses.

- How do you implement least-privilege access and just-in-time (JIT) administration with automated credential revocation?
  - Strategic Context: Excessive privileges and standing admin access expand attack surface. JIT and least privilege minimize exposure and lateral movement risk.

- Can you detect and respond to identity compromise (credential theft, token abuse, MFA fatigue) within minutes?
  - Strategic Context: Attackers move from credential theft to business impact in minutes. Detection and revocation must happen at the same speed.

- How do you govern privileged access management (PAM), including session monitoring, approval workflows, and emergency "break-glass" access?
  - Strategic Context: Privileged accounts are the keys to the kingdom. PAM ensures these credentials are monitored, controlled, and revocable.

- What is your approach to Zero Trust architecture, including conditional access policies and continuous identity verification in the SOC?
  - Strategic Context: Zero Trust assumes breach and verifies every access. It is the architecture that enforces identity-first security at runtime.


#### Domain 4: Threat Detection & Response Speed

Why This Domain Matters: Attack timelines have collapsed. When attackers can reach business impact before your first triage call, reaction time becomes board-level risk. MTTD and MTTR are now strategic metrics.

##### Strategic Questions:

- What is your mean time to detect (MTTD) for critical threats, and how do you differentiate by attack vector (e.g., identity, ransomware, lateral movement)?
  - Strategic Context: MTTD is a board-level metric. If detection takes longer than attacker breakout time, containment becomes recovery.

- What is your mean time to respond (MTTR) and mean time to contain (MTTC), and how are these measured and reported?
  - Strategic Context: MTTR and MTTC measure resilience. Long response times increase data loss, operational disruption, and regulatory exposure.

- How do you achieve detection and containment at "machine speed" (minutes, not hours or days)?
  - Strategic Context: Human-speed response cannot keep pace with machine-speed attacks. Automation is not optional; it is a control.

- What percentage of alerts are true positives, and how do you manage false positive rates to prevent analyst fatigue?
  - Strategic Context: High false positive rates cause analyst fatigue, missed alerts, and delayed response. Accuracy directly impacts effectiveness.

- Do you have pre-authorized, automated containment actions (e.g., disable account, isolate device, revoke token, block lateral movement)?
  - Strategic Context: Pre-authorized containment actions compress decision cycles from hours to seconds, limiting blast radius.

- How do you measure and report on "breakout time" – the interval between initial compromise and lateral movement?
  - Strategic Context: Breakout time is the attacker's advantage. Measuring it reveals whether your detection is fast enough to matter.


#### Domain 5: AI & Automation

Why This Domain Matters: AI transforms cybersecurity at unprecedented speed. Attackers use AI to scale phishing and intrusion; defenders must respond with AI-driven detection, predictive modelling, and automated response—cutting reaction times from hours to seconds.

##### Strategic Questions:

- How do you leverage AI to accelerate threat hunting, triage, investigation, and response?
  - Strategic Context: AI compresses triage, hunting, and investigation from hours to minutes, enabling machine-speed defense.

- What is your approach to AI governance, including oversight of AI tools used internally and by clients (e.g., shadow AI detection)?
  - Strategic Context: Unmanaged AI tools (shadow AI) create data leakage, compliance risk, and governance gaps. Visibility and oversight are essential.

- Can you describe your use of AI agents (e.g., Security Copilot) and how they augment human decision-making without replacing accountability?
  - Strategic Context: AI agents augment human expertise but do not replace accountability. The question is how, not if, AI is used

- How do you ensure AI-driven decisions are explainable, auditable, and grounded in trustworthy data?
  - Strategic Context: Explainability ensures AI decisions can be audited, defended, and trusted by regulators, boards, and stakeholders.

- What safeguards and human-in-the-loop processes are in place to prevent over-reliance on automation?
  - Strategic Context: Over-reliance on automation without human oversight creates new risks. Safeguards ensure AI serves defense, not just efficiency.


#### Domain 6: Threat Intelligence & Collective Defense

Why This Domain Matters: Adversaries operate as interconnected networks, sharing tools and tactics at global scale. Defenders must embrace collective defense—trusted, operational sharing with peers and hyperscalers—to match attacker speed.

##### Strategic Questions:

- What threat intelligence feeds and sources do you consume, and how is this intelligence operationalized in real-time?
  - Strategic Context: Generic threat intelligence is noise. Operationalized intelligence is contextual, timely, and actionable within your environment.

- How do you participate in collective defense initiatives, including trusted information sharing with peers, hyperscalers, ISACs, and government CERTs?
  - Strategic Context: Attackers share tools and tactics globally. Defenders must do the same. Collective defense turns intelligence into a control

- Can you provide examples of how shared threat intelligence has accelerated detection or prevention for your clients?
  - Strategic Context: Shared intelligence accelerates detection and prevention. Real-world examples demonstrate whether this is operational or aspirational.

- Do you use standardized formats (e.g., STIX/TAXII) for bi-directional intelligence sharing, and how is this integrated into orchestration and response workflows?
  - Strategic Context: Standardized formats enable machine-speed sharing and orchestration. Manual processes create delay and limit value.


#### Domain 7: Incident Response & Resilience

Why This Domain Matters: Assume breach and design for continuity. Automate detection and containment, reduce decision latency, and pre-decide what can fail so the business can keep running during an incident.

##### Strategic Questions:

- How do you support "assume breach" planning, including pre-deciding what can fail and what must stay operational during an incident?
  - Strategic Context: Assume breach planning defines what must stay up and what can fail. This is a leadership decision, not a technical one.

- What is your incident response playbook maturity, and how do you ensure tested, rehearsed coordination with our internal teams?
  - Strategic Context: Incident response under pressure is chaotic. Tested playbooks and rehearsed coordination ensure speed and clarity.

- Can you produce accurate incident timelines, impact assessments, and evidence packages to meet regulatory notification deadlines (e.g., 24-72 hours)?
  - Strategic Context: Regulatory notification deadlines are strict (24-72 hours). Failure to report accurately and on time compounds legal exposure.

- How do you support business continuity and disaster recovery, including backup isolation, recovery testing, and dependency mapping?
  - Strategic Context: Resilience is the ability to sustain operations under attack. Backup isolation and recovery testing prove resilience is real.

- What is your approach to post-incident learning, including root cause analysis, lessons learned, and continuous improvement?
  - Strategic Context: Post-incident learning turns disruption into improvement. Without it, organizations repeat the same mistakes.


#### Domain 8: Data Ownership & Sovereignty

Why This Domain Matters: Data ownership, residency, and sovereignty are legal and operational imperatives. Ambiguity creates risk during audits, breaches, and contract termination. Data governance must address who controls the data, where it lives, and under what conditions it can be shared.

##### Strategic Questions:

- Who owns the data ingested into your SOC platform, and what are the contractual terms for data retention, access, and deletion?
  - Strategic Context: Data ownership defines control, liability, and exit rights. Ambiguity creates risk during audits, breaches, and contract termination.

- Where is our data stored (geography, cloud tenancy model), and how do you ensure compliance with data residency and sovereignty requirements?
  - Strategic Context: Data residency and sovereignty are legal requirements in many jurisdictions. Non-compliance can block business operations.

- How do you protect the confidentiality and integrity of our telemetry data, including encryption at rest and in transit?
  - Strategic Context: Telemetry data contains sensitive information. Encryption and access controls ensure confidentiality and integrity.

- Under what circumstances can our data be shared with third parties (e.g., threat intelligence partners, law enforcement), and what approval processes are in place?
  - Strategic Context: Data sharing without governance creates compliance, privacy, and reputational risk. Approval processes protect the organization.


#### Domain 9: Business Alignment & Reporting

Why This Domain Matters: CISOs sit at the intersection of risk, technology, and business accountability. The SOC must translate security performance into language that boards and executives understand: risk reduction, resilience posture, and business impact.

##### Strategic Questions:

- How do you translate SOC performance into business risk language that boards and executives can understand?
  - Strategic Context: Boards do not speak SOC language. Translation into business risk is the CISO's responsibility and the SOC's deliverable.

- What KPIs and metrics do you report to CISO and board level (e.g., risk reduction, exposure trends, resilience posture)?
  - Strategic Context: KPIs must measure outcomes (risk reduction, resilience) not just activity (alerts, tickets). Metrics without meaning are vanity.

- How do you align SOC operations with our critical business services and crown-jewel assets?
  - Strategic Context: Not all assets are equal. Aligning SOC focus with critical services ensures resources protect what matters most.

- What is your escalation model for high-severity incidents, and how quickly do we receive executive-level notifications?

- Strategic Context: Executive notification during incidents must be fast, clear, and accurate. Delay or confusion erodes trust and increases impact.

#### Domain 10: Operational Maturity

Why This Domain Matters: Operational maturity ensures the SOC can deliver consistent, high-quality service 24/7/365. Certifications, staffing models, continuous improvement practices, and analyst well-being directly impact detection quality and service reliability.

##### Strategic Questions:

- What certifications and accreditations does your SOC hold (e.g., ISO 27001, SOC 2, CREST, NIST CSF)?
  - Strategic Context: Certifications provide assurance of process maturity, control effectiveness, and third-party validation.

- How do you ensure 24/7/365 coverage, and what is your staffing model (locations, shift patterns, expertise levels)?
  - Strategic Context: Threats do not respect business hours. Continuous coverage ensures detection and response do not have gaps.

- What is your approach to continuous improvement, including regular control testing, tabletop exercises, and red/purple team engagements?
  - Strategic Context: Security is not static. Continuous improvement, testing, and adversary emulation ensure defences evolve faster than threats.

- How do you measure and manage analyst productivity, burnout, and retention to ensure consistent service quality?
  - Strategic Context: Analyst burnout degrades detection quality and increases turnover. Operational health directly impacts service delivery.

- What is your client onboarding and offboarding process, including knowledge transfer, documentation, and transition planning?
  - Strategic Context: Onboarding and offboarding define the client experience. Poor transitions create knowledge loss and operational disruption.


### Metrics

This chapter offers a set of idea metrics for “Security at Machine Speed.” It is not intended to be exhaustive, but to provide a starting point for discussion.

#### Detection & Response Speed Metrics

- **Mean Time to Detect (MTTD)** - Lagging
  Average time between threat entry and detection. Industry median for internal detection is 16 days, but modern attacks exfiltrate data within 5-8 hours. Target: \< 24 hours (general); \< 1 hour (high-risk). This metric directly validates whether your SOC operates at machine speed.

- **Mean Time to Respond (MTTR)** - Lagging
  Time from detection to containment. Organizations with automated response workflows achieve 45-55% faster MTTR. Target: \< 1 hour (critical); \< 24 hours (high-priority). This measures the effectiveness of automated playbooks and pre-authorized containment actions described in Chapter 3.

- **Mean Time to Contain (MTTC)** - Lagging
  Time to stop threat spread once detected. Target: \< 15 minutes (critical incidents). This validates whether isolation, credential revocation, and token blocking happen automatically.

- **Breakout Time Prevention Rate** - Leading
  Percentage of incidents contained before lateral movement. Breakout time has collapsed to under one hour. Target: \> 85%. Measures Zero Trust effectiveness and identity perimeter strength.

- **Automated Response Coverage** - Leading
  Percentage of incidents with automated containment actions (device isolation, account disabling, token revocation). Target: \> 60%. Validates that SOC operates at machine speed with human-on-the-loop, not human-in-the-loop.

#### Identity & Access Management Metrics

- **MFA Adoption Rate** - Leading
  Percentage of users/accounts with MFA enabled. Target: 100% (all users); 100% (privileged). Identity is the control plane for Zero Trust.

- **Phishing-Resistant MFA Coverage** - Leading
  Percentage using FIDO2, certificate-based, or other phishing-resistant methods. Target: 100% (privileged); \> 80% (all users). Standard MFA is vulnerable to advanced phishing and MFA fatigue attacks.

- **Passwordless Authentication Coverage** - Leading
  Percentage of authentication events using passwordless methods (Windows Hello, FIDO2, biometrics). Target: \> 70% within 12 months. Eliminates credential theft as an attack vector.

- **Privileged Accounts with JIT Access** - Leading
  Percentage using just-in-time (JIT) and privileged access management (PAM). Target: \> 90%. Eliminates standing administrative access, reducing the attack window.

- **Privileged Account Usage Monitoring** - Leading
  Percentage of privileged sessions logged, monitored, and analysed for anomalies. Target: 100%. Unusual patterns (off-hours access, privilege escalation) indicate compromise.

- **Orphaned Accounts Detected** - Lagging
  Active accounts without associated user records (departed employees, contractors). Target: \< 2% of total accounts. These accounts are prime targets for attackers seeking easy access.

- **Time to Deprovision Access** - Lagging
  Time to revoke access after role change or departure. Target: \< 1 hour (automated); same-day (manual). Delayed deprovisioning creates exploitable windows.

- **Failed Login Attempt Rate** - Lagging
  Number of failed authentications per period, with threshold-based alerting. Spikes indicate brute-force or credential-stuffing attacks.

- **Conditional Access Policy Coverage** - Leading
  Percentage of applications protected by risk-based conditional access policies. Target: 100% (critical apps); \> 95% (all apps). Enforces Zero Trust verification at every access request.

#### Governance & Risk Management Metrics

- **Governance Review Frequency** - Leading
  Cadence of board and executive cyber risk reviews. Target: Quarterly (board); Monthly (executive). Validates continuous governance, not annual checkbox compliance.

- **Control Effectiveness Score** - Lagging
  Percentage of security controls validated as effective through testing. Target: \> 95%. Answers the question: "Are controls delivering the intended outcome?".

- **Risk Acceptance Documentation Rate** - Leading
  Percentage of accepted risks with documented business owner sign-off. Target: 100%. One CISO's rule: "Risk acceptance should be signed by the person who would stand before cameras to explain the decision if it materialized". This ensures accountability.

- **Crown Jewel Asset Identification** - Leading
  Percentage of critical business assets identified, mapped, and prioritized. Target: 100% (typically 1-3% of total assets). Focuses protection on what truly matters.

- **Risk Assessment Cycle Time** - Lagging
  Time to complete risk assessment and reach decision. Target: \< 7 days (critical); \< 30 days (standard). Slow governance cannot keep pace with machine-speed attacks.

- **Third-Party Risk Assessments Completed** - Leading
  Percentage of critical vendors with current security assessments. Target: 100% (critical); \> 80% (all vendors). Supply chain is part of your attack surface.

- **Policy Violation Rate** - Lagging
  Number of security policy violations detected. Target: Declining trend; \< 5 per 1,000 users/month. Measures policy enforcement and security culture effectiveness.

#### SOC Performance Metrics

- **Alert Triage Automation Rate** - Leading
  Percentage of alerts initially triaged by AI/automation before analyst review. Target: \> 70%. Frees analysts from manual correlation for high-value threat hunting.

- **False Positive Rate** - Lagging
  Percentage of alerts that are false positives. Target: \< 10%. High false-positive rates cause alert fatigue and obscure real threats.

- **True Positive Detection Rate** - Lagging
  Percentage of actual threats successfully detected. Target: \> 95%. Validates detection effectiveness and security graph value.

- **Incident Response Playbook Coverage** - Leading
  Percentage of incident types with documented, automated playbooks. Target: \> 80%. Enables standardized, machine-speed response.

- **Analyst Time on Threat Hunting** - Leading
  Percentage of time spent on proactive hunting vs reactive firefighting. Target: \> 30%. SOC teams spending less than 30% on hunting are "essentially firefighting all day". Indicates SOC maturity.

- **Security Data Lake Coverage** - Leading
  Percentage of environment with telemetry feeding the unified data lake. Target: \> 95%. The data lake is the "live feed" that keeps the security graph current.

- **SOC Transparency Score** - Leading
  Visibility of SOC operations for business stakeholders through dashboards and reporting. Target: Monthly reporting; real-time dashboards. Governance requires visibility into operations.

- **Security Graph Relationship Coverage** - Leading
  Percentage of assets with mapped relationships and dependencies. Target: \> 90%. The security graph connects identities, systems, data, and business services—enabling graph-based defense.

#### Data Governance Metrics

- **Crown Jewel Data Classification Rate** - Leading
  Percentage of critical data assets classified and labelled. Target: 100%. You cannot protect what you cannot see.

- **Data Protection Policy Coverage** - Leading
  Percentage of classified data with DLP policies, encryption, and access controls applied. Target: 100% (crown jewels); \> 95% (all sensitive data).

- **DLP Incident Rate** - Lagging
  Number of data loss prevention incidents per period. Target: Declining trend; \< 10 per month. Measures exfiltration prevention effectiveness.

- **Data Access Anomaly Detection** - Lagging
  Number of unusual data access patterns detected (bulk downloads, off-hours access, privilege misuse). Behavioral analytics detect insider threats and lateral movement.

- **Sensitive Data Discovery Coverage** - Leading
  Percentage of environment scanned for sensitive data (including shadow IT and forgotten repositories). Target: 100% (quarterly full scan). Discovers unmanaged data.

- **Data Retention Compliance** - Leading
  Percentage of data assets with documented retention policies and lifecycle management. Target: 100%. Required for regulatory compliance (GDPR, NIS2, DORA).

#### AI Governance Metrics

- **Sanctioned AI Agents with Identity** - Leading
  Number of approved AI agents with managed identities and lifecycle controls. Target: 100% of all AI agents. AI agents are employees—they require identity, monitoring, and governance.

- **Shadow AI Instances Detected** - Lagging
  Number of unsanctioned AI tools/agents discovered. Target: \< 5% of total AI usage. One in five organizations experienced a breach linked to shadow AI last year.

- **AI Policy Violations Detected** - Lagging
  Number of AI usage policy breaches (unauthorized data use, unapproved models). Target: Declining trend; \< 2 per month. Enforces AI guardrails.

- **AI Model Audit Coverage** - Leading
  Percentage of production AI models with documented audits (performance, fairness, data provenance). Target: 100% (high-risk); \> 90% (all models). Required by EU AI Act and other regulations.

- **AI Agent Lifecycle Compliance** - Leading
  Percentage of AI agents with complete lifecycle management (provisioning, monitoring, deprovisioning). Target: 100%. Non-human identities require the same governance as humans.

- **AI Training Data Governance** - Leading
  Percentage of AI models with documented data provenance, quality, and privacy compliance. Target: 100% (high-risk); \> 80% (all models). Data governance extends to AI training data.

#### Collective Defense & Collaboration Metrics

- **Active Threat Intel Partnerships** - Leading
  Number of active threat intelligence sharing relationships (peers, hyperscalers, ISACs). Target: \> 5 (peers); \> 2 (hyperscalers). Collective defense is a control, not just a concept.

- **Time to Share Threat Indicators** - Lagging
  Average time from detection to sharing indicators with partners. Target: \< 1 hour (critical); \< 24 hours (standard). Rapid sharing blocks threats before they spread across organizations.

- **Threat Intel Integration Rate** - Leading
  Percentage of external threat intelligence automatically integrated into SOC workflows. Target: \> 80%. Automates enrichment of security graph with threat intelligence overlay.

- **Joint Exercise Participation** - Leading
  Number of collaborative security exercises with peers, governments, hyperscalers. Target: \> 4 per year (quarterly). Builds trust and coordination before crisis.

- **Indicators Received vs Shared Ratio** - Leading
  Ratio of threat indicators received to indicators shared. Target: Balanced reciprocity (0.8-1.2). True partnership requires mutual contribution, not just consumption.

#### Resilience & Recovery Metrics

- **Recovery Time Objective (RTO) Compliance** - Leading
  Percentage of critical services meeting RTO targets (maximum acceptable downtime). Target: 100%. Defines what "operational resilience" means in practice.

- **Recovery Point Objective (RPO) Compliance** - Leading
  Percentage of critical data meeting RPO targets (maximum acceptable data loss). Target: 100%. Defines acceptable loss thresholds.

- **Backup Testing Frequency** - Leading
  Frequency of backup restore testing (not just backup completion). Target: Monthly (critical); Quarterly (all systems). Untested backups are not backups.

- **Backup Success Rate** - Lagging
  Percentage of scheduled backups completed successfully. Target: \> 99.5%. Foundation for ransomware resilience.

- **Systems with Defined Loss Thresholds** - Leading
  Percentage of critical systems with explicit business-approved acceptable loss definitions. Target: 100%. Pre-decided tradeoffs enable fast crisis decisions.

- **Time to Recover Critical Services** - Lagging
  Actual time to restore critical services during incidents. Target: Within RTO (\< 4 hours typical). Validates recovery capability under pressure.

- **Incident Containment Success Rate** - Lagging
  Percentage of incidents contained without business impact. Target: \> 85%. Measures effective response and machine-speed containment.

#### Cultural & Behavioral Metrics

- **Security Training Completion Rate** - Leading
  Percentage completing mandatory training. Target: \> 95% (annual); 100% (onboarding). Baseline for security awareness.

- **Phishing Simulation Click Rate** - Lagging
  Percentage clicking simulated phishing emails. Target: \< 5% (declining trend). An AI-generated spear-phishing campaign achieved a 54% click rate—on par with human experts. Training must address this.

- **Phishing Simulation Reporting Rate** - Leading
  Percentage reporting simulated phishing attempts. Target: \> 70%. Active defence culture—employees as sensors, not just targets.

- **Employee-Reported Security Incidents** - Leading
  Number of security incidents reported by employees. Target: Increasing trend. More reports indicate better awareness and psychological safety.

- **Time to Report Suspected Incidents** - Lagging
  Average time from suspicion to incident report. Target: \< 1 hour. Early reporting enables early containment.

- **Security Champion Engagement** - Leading
  Number of active security champions across business units. Target: \> 1 per 100 employees. Distributed security culture and business enablement.

#### Red Team & Validation Metrics

- **Successful Red Team Attack Rate** - Lagging
  Percentage of red team attacks achieving objectives. Target: \< 30% (declining over time). Validates real-world defense effectiveness.

- **Time to Detect Red Team Activity** - Lagging
  Average time to detect simulated attacks. Target: \< 24 hours (improving to \< 1 hour). Correlates with real threat detection capability.

- **Control Validation Coverage** - Leading
  Percentage of security controls tested via simulation annually. Target: \> 80%. Continuous validation, not one-time assessment.

- **Purple Team Exercise Frequency** - Leading
  Number of collaborative red/blue exercises. Target: \> 6 per year (bi-monthly). Builds SOC capability through deliberate practice.

- **Remediation Rate of Red Team Findings** - Lagging
  Percentage of findings remediated within SLA. Target: \> 90% within 30 days. Closes gaps and drives improvement.

#### Vulnerability Management Metrics

- **Critical Vulnerability Patch Time** - Lagging
  Average time to patch critical vulnerabilities. Target: \< 7 days (critical); \< 30 days (high). Unpatched vulnerabilities are easy wins for attackers.

- **Patch Compliance Rate** - Lagging
  Percentage of systems with current patches. Target: \> 95%. Many organisations set 95-99% targets.

- **Zero-Day Response Time** - Lagging
  Time to implement mitigations for zero-day threats. Target: \< 24 hours. Machine-speed response to emerging threats.
